p. As part of my "Computer Graphics class":https://www.student.cs.uwaterloo.ca/~cs488/ at the University of Waterloo, I took on the project to build a ray tracer and implement a number of visual effects to improve the realism of the rendered image.

p. The starting point for this project was a 1984 paper by Cook called "Distributed Ray Tracing":http://www.eng.utah.edu/~cs6965/papers/p137-cook.pdf. This paper describes an interesting approach to ray tracing whereas rays are distributed following a desired distribution in a given dimension to render certain fuzzy effects. For example, to generate gloss, multiple reflected rays are traced randomly in a cone shaped distribution based on the "BRDF":http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bidirectional_reflectance_distribution_function of the surface hit by the ray. The rays are then averaged to compute the final result. This is a simple solution to many of the problems ray tracing was facing at the time and allowed for the rendering of then complex effects such as motion blur or depth of field.


p. However, this solution becomes problematic as soon as more complex scenes need to be rendered. It is, at its core, an exponential process; each ray, as it intersects with a surface, creates _n_ new rays. This problem becomes increasingly noticeable when reflection and refraction are introduced as they are recursive processes. This is where Monte Carlo path tracing becomes handy. Instead of generating _n_ new rays everytime an intersection is found, another solution is to generate many rays at the camera and have each follow a random walk through the scene. When a surface is hit, the new direction of the ray is determined randomly following the distribution function at the surface and no new ray is created. As the number of camera rays increases, the quality of the image will increase without an exponential explosion in the number of rays. This approach was implemented and used to render scenes with a large number of translucent objects where internal reflection is particularly problematic.
